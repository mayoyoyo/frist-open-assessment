# Paper Synthesis

These five papers form a deliberate arc, and each one dismantles an assumption the previous one left standing.

**JEDI (Scaling CUA Grounding)** starts with a claim: "GUI grounding...remains a critical bottleneck in computer use agent development" (Abstract). A 7B model trained on 4M synthetic UI examples, paired with o3 as planner, hits 51% on OSWorld — beating monolithic models 10x its size. The planner "receives high-level instructions and, at each step, predicts the next low-level natural language instruction"; JEDI "then takes these low-level instructions and predicts the concrete actions to execute" (Section 3.2). Separate the grounder from the planner. Data scaling shows "no sign of saturation" (Section 4.2). Problem solved?

**AutoWebWorld** picks up what JEDI ignores — where do training *environments* come from? It models websites as FSMs where "the environment's internal state [is] a pair of page identity and a page signature, st = (pt, sigma_t)" (Section 3). BFS produces trajectories that are "correct at the FSM level by construction" (Section 4.3), "without relying on external judges" (Section 4.4) — at $0.04 each versus $0.15-$1.00 for real-world pipelines (Section 4.5). This is exactly what `crawler.js` implements: `hashState(pathname, stateObj)` mirrors AutoWebWorld's `(page_id, signature)` pair; the parallel BFS work queue is the same expansion strategy. AutoWebWorld validates transfer — training on synthetic websites yields 27.4% on real-world WebVoyager, with "consistent improvement as synthetic data scales" (Section 6). But it treats trajectory correctness as binary: "a trajectory is accepted only if all steps can be carried out successfully" (Section 4.4).

**InfiniteWeb** challenges that binary thinking. Its core contribution isn't environment generation — it's reward design. Dense checkpoint evaluators are "capable of assessing partial completion rather than only binary success/failure" (Section 3.5), enabling learning from "767 tasks compared to 174 with binary reward — a 4.4x increase" in effective training signal (Section 4.6, Figure 7). This exposes a gap in this repo: `env.py` uses BFS-distance shaping (+0.1 per edge closer to goal), which is topological, not semantic. The JetBlue clone's `BookingState` already tracks each form field — converting those into weighted checkpoints would directly apply InfiniteWeb's finding. For the hardest task (traveler-to-seats, 20 steps through a multi-field form), binary reward means the agent either navigates the entire form correctly or learns nothing.

**OpenCUA** then delivers the sharpest result in the set: "directly finetuning vision-language action models on our 27K trajectories leads to poor performance (4.4% success rate on OSWorld)" (Section 3). The same data with reflective chain-of-thought reaches 45.0% (Section 4.2) — 10x from annotation format alone. Critically, OpenCUA is pure SFT — all models are produced via "supervised fine-tuning" (Section 4.1) with no RL component. The key is CoT synthesis that "explicitly injects planning, memory, and reflection into the per-step reasoning process" (Section 3.1). Without CoT, "training on state-action pairs alone yields limited performance gains even as the dataset size scales" (Section 3) — the flat blue line in Figure 1. More trajectories from `crawler.js` won't help unless each step is annotated with reasoning. The repo has the ingredients (screenshots per state, annotation labels, computable state diffs) but no synthesis pipeline. The Pass@1-to-Pass@3 gap (45% to 53.2%) suggests "ample headroom for future post-training" (Section 4.2) — a direct nod to RL as the logical next step after strong SFT.

**GroundCUA** completes the arc by looping back to challenge JEDI. Training on "only 700K instructions, which is far below multi-million-sample corpora used by prior work (e.g., JEDI 9M)" still outperforms on every benchmark (Section 5.1). The differentiator is density: "averaging 64 per screenshot, more than three times that of OS-Atlas" and far beyond JEDI's 7 (Section 2), because "JEDI, despite its scale, has sparser annotations due to its reliance on synthetic data" (Appendix A.4). The sharper finding is the RL-gain inversion (Figure 3): "models trained with GroundCUA during SFT show the smallest performance gains from RL...suggesting that SFT with GroundCUA already provides highly informative supervision, leaving fewer errors for RL to correct" (Section 5.2).

**The collective argument** reveals three bottlenecks, each only visible once the previous is solved. First, the **environment bottleneck**: you can't train without controllable, verifiable environments — AutoWebWorld's FSM-BFS pattern (and this repo's crawler) solves it. Second, the **signal bottleneck**: having environments isn't enough. Raw trajectories don't scale (OpenCUA's flat curve), and binary rewards waste 75% of training signal (InfiniteWeb). You need dense checkpoint rewards *and* reasoning annotations to extract useful signal from the environments you built. Third, the **architecture bottleneck**: even with good signal, a monolithic model wastes it. JEDI and GroundCUA independently show a 3B grounder + frontier planner beats monolithic 72B models — and GroundCUA shows that quality SFT on dense annotations captures most of the learnable signal before RL even enters the picture.

**The tensions** are real. Synthetic data works for environments but loses to human data for grounding. SFT and RL are sequential, not competing, but where to draw the line depends on SFT quality. Every paper assumes deterministic transitions — `crawler.js` does too — but real websites aren't deterministic. And the grounder-planner split is validated at 3B+o3 scale but never compared against a monolithic model at equal training compute.

**Where this repo sits:** it implements the first step well (FSM-BFS crawling). What's missing, in order of leverage: dense checkpoint rewards from the clone's existing state tracking, a CoT synthesis pipeline for the state graph trajectories, and bridging the action-space gap — `env.py` trains on annotation indices while `eval/agent.py` evaluates on pixel coordinates, which are fundamentally incompatible action spaces that no amount of training can reconcile.
